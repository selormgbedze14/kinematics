Course Overview

Kinematics is the branch of physics that describes how objects move — without asking why (that's dynamics/Newton's Laws). It gives us the mathematical language to describe position, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time.

Chapter 01
Distance & Displacement

These two concepts are often confused but are fundamentally different:

PropertyDistanceDisplacement
TypeScalar (magnitude only)Vector (magnitude + direction)
DefinitionTotal path length travelledStraight-line change in position
Symbolds or x
Unitmetres (m)metres (m) + direction
Can be negative?NeverYes (opposite to reference direction)
🔑 Classic Example
A runner completes one full lap of a 400 m track.
Distance = 400 m (total path length)
Displacement = 0 m (start and end at same position)

Position & Reference Points

Displacement always requires a reference point (origin). Moving 5 m to the right might be written as +5 m; returning 3 m left gives a net displacement of +2 m — even though the distance travelled is 8 m.

s = x_final − x_initial
Displacement = final position minus initial position.
Positive = in chosen positive direction; Negative = opposite direction.

Chapter 02
Speed & Velocity
Speed = distance / time    v = d / t
Scalar — how fast, no direction. Units: m/s
Velocity = displacement / time    v = s / t
Vector — how fast AND in which direction. Units: m/s with direction.

Average vs Instantaneous

AverageInstantaneous
Speedtotal distance ÷ total timespeed at a single instant (speedometer)
Velocitytotal displacement ÷ total timetangent to displacement–time graph
📌 Key Distinction
A car driving around a roundabout at constant 30 mph has constant speed but changing velocity — because its direction continuously changes. Direction is part of velocity!

Example — Sprinter

A sprinter runs 100 m in 9.58 s. What is their average speed? (Usain Bolt, 2009)

v = d/t = 100 / 9.58 = 10.44 m/s ≈ 37.6 km/h

Since the race is a straight line, speed ≈ velocity magnitude.

Chapter 03
Acceleration
a = (v − u) / t   =   Δv / Δt
a = acceleration (m/s²)  |  v = final velocity  |  u = initial velocity  |  t = time taken
Acceleration is a vector — it has both magnitude and direction.

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. It can be positive (speeding up in positive direction), negative (slowing down = deceleration), or zero (constant velocity).

Sign Convention

⚠️ Sign Convention (critical!)
Always define a positive direction at the start of a problem.
a > 0: accelerating in positive direction
a < 0: decelerating (if moving +) or accelerating in − direction
a = 0: constant velocity (or at rest)

Example — Car Braking

A car travelling at 30 m/s brakes to a stop in 5 s. Find the acceleration.

a = (v − u) / t = (0 − 30) / 5 = −6 m/s²

Negative sign shows deceleration (opposing motion). The magnitude is 6 m/s².

Chapter 04
SUVAT Equations

The five SUVAT equations describe uniformly accelerated motion (constant acceleration). They connect five variables: s, u, v, a, t.

s = displacement (m)  |  u = initial velocity (m/s)
v = final velocity (m/s)  |  a = acceleration (m/s²)  |  t = time (s)
You always know 3 of the 5 variables — pick the equation with your 3 knowns + 1 unknown.
v = u + at
Missing: s
s = ut + ½at²
Missing: v
v² = u² + 2as
Missing: t
s = ½(u+v)t
Missing: a
s = vt − ½at²
Missing: u

Example — Train Stopping

A train travelling at 50 m/s decelerates at 2 m/s². How far does it travel before stopping?

Known: u = 50 m/s, v = 0, a = −2 m/s². Find s. Missing: t → use v² = u² + 2as

0 = 2500 + 2(−2)s → 4s = 2500 → s = 625 m

Chapter 05
Uniform Motion

Uniform motion means motion at constant velocity — zero acceleration. The object covers equal distances in equal time intervals.

s = v × t    (a = 0)
Displacement = constant velocity × time.
This is a special case of SUVAT with a = 0, so u = v throughout.

Characteristics

  • Displacement–time graph: Straight line (non-zero slope)
  • Velocity–time graph: Horizontal line
  • Acceleration–time graph: Flat at zero

Chapter 06
Uniform Acceleration

Uniform acceleration means constant acceleration. Velocity changes by the same amount each second. This is the most common type of motion in kinematics problems.

Key Features

  • s–t graph: Parabola (curved)
  • v–t graph: Straight diagonal line
  • a–t graph: Horizontal line (non-zero constant)
  • Equal velocity increments per second

Example — Sports Car

A sports car accelerates from rest at 4.5 m/s² for 6 seconds. Find final speed and distance covered.

u = 0, a = 4.5, t = 6

v = u + at = 0 + 4.5×6 = 27 m/s (97.2 km/h)

s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½(4.5)(36) = 81 m

Chapter 07
Free Fall & Gravity

Free fall is the motion of an object under gravity alone — no air resistance, no thrust. Near Earth's surface, all objects accelerate downward at the same rate regardless of mass.

g = 9.81 m/s² (downward)
Standard gravitational acceleration at Earth's surface.
In free fall: a = −g = −9.81 m/s² (taking upward as positive)
Moon: g ≈ 1.62 m/s²  |  Mars: g ≈ 3.72 m/s²

SUVAT for Free Fall

Apply the 5 SUVAT equations with a = ±9.81 m/s². Always define your positive direction first (usually upward positive is most intuitive):

ScenariouaKey equation
Dropped from rest0−9.81s = ½gt² (downward)
Thrown upward at u+u−9.81v = u − gt at top: v=0
Time to hit ground0+9.81t = √(2h/g)

⚠️ Common Mistake
When an object is thrown upward, at the maximum height the velocity is zero but acceleration is still −9.81 m/s² downward. Gravity never "switches off"!
Example — Ball Thrown Upward

A ball is thrown straight up at 20 m/s. Find the maximum height and total time of flight.

At max height, v = 0. Using v² = u² + 2as: 0 = 400 + 2(−9.81)s → s = 20.4 m

Time up: t = (v−u)/a = (0−20)/(−9.81) = 2.04 s. Total flight = 2 × 2.04 = 4.08 s

Chapter 08
Projectile Motion
🔑 Key Principle
Horizontal and vertical motions are completely independent.
Horizontal: constant velocity (no air resistance) — uniform motion
Vertical: constant acceleration g downward — free fall
x = u·cosθ · t     y = u·sinθ · t − ½g·t²
u = launch speed  |  θ = launch angle  |  g = 9.81 m/s²
Range = u²·sin(2θ)/g  |  Max height = u²·sin²θ/(2g)  |  Time of flight = 2u·sinθ/g

Maximum Range

For a given launch speed from flat ground, θ = 45° gives maximum range. Complementary angles (e.g. 30° and 60°) give the same range but different trajectories.

Example — Football Kick

A football is kicked at 25 m/s at 30° to the horizontal. Find the range.

Range = u²·sin(2θ)/g = 625 × sin(60°) / 9.81 = 625 × 0.866 / 9.81 = 55.1 m

Chapter 09
Displacement–Time Graphs
🔑 Key Rule
The gradient (slope) of a displacement–time graph = velocity
Steep slope → high velocity. Horizontal → at rest. Negative slope → moving backwards.
Graph ShapeMotion
Horizontal lineAt rest (v = 0)
Straight diagonal lineConstant velocity (uniform motion)
Curved (concave up)Accelerating (velocity increasing)
Curved (concave down)Decelerating (velocity decreasing)
Negative slopeMoving in negative direction
Returns to x-axisBack at starting point

Reading the Gradient

For a curved s–t graph, the instantaneous velocity at any point equals the gradient of the tangent line at that point. This is the derivative ds/dt.

v = ds/dt = gradient of s–t graph
For a straight section: gradient = Δs/Δt = rise/run
Chapter 10
Velocity–Time Graphs

The velocity–time graph is the most informative kinematic graph — it gives you both acceleration (gradient) and displacement (area).

1. Gradient of v–t graph = acceleration
2. Area under v–t graph = displacement

Chapter 11
Acceleration–Time Graphs
🔑 Key Rule
Area under an acceleration–time graph = change in velocity (Δv)
Graph ShapeMotion
Horizontal line at 0Constant velocity (or at rest)
Horizontal line above 0Uniform acceleration
Horizontal line below 0Uniform deceleration
Varying lineNon-uniform acceleration

Connecting All Three Graphs

s(t) → differentiate → v(t) → differentiate → a(t)
a(t) → integrate → v(t) → integrate → s(t)
Differentiation gives rate of change (going "down" the chain).
Integration gives accumulated quantity (going "up" the chain).

Chapter 12
2D Motion & Vectors

Real-world motion is rarely in a straight line. For 2D kinematics, we resolve vectors into components and treat each axis independently.

v_x = v·cosθ     v_y = v·sinθ
Resolve any velocity into horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components.
Apply kinematics separately to each direction.

Combining Perpendicular Components

|v| = √(v_x² + v_y²)     θ = arctan(v_y / v_x)
Magnitude and direction of resultant vector from components.

Relative Motion

Velocity depends on the observer's reference frame. A passenger on a train moving at 30 m/s throws a ball forward at 10 m/s — a stationary observer sees the ball at 40 m/s.

v_AB = v_A − v_B
Velocity of A relative to B = velocity of A minus velocity of B (in same reference frame).
Chapter 13
Review & Quiz

Test your mastery of kinematics. Click an answer to reveal feedback.

1. A car travels 100 m east, then 60 m west. What is its displacement?
✅ Displacement = 100 − 60 = 40 m east. Distance = 160 m. Don't confuse them!
2. A ball is thrown upward at 15 m/s. What is its acceleration at the highest point?
✅ Gravity always acts downward at 9.81 m/s² regardless of velocity. At the peak, v = 0 but a = g downward.
3. The gradient of a velocity–time graph represents:
✅ Gradient of v–t = acceleration. Area under v–t = displacement. Both are critical rules!
4. An object starts at rest and accelerates at 5 m/s² for 4 s. How far does it travel?
✅ s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½(5)(16) = 40 m. Remember u = 0 (starts from rest).
5. A projectile is launched at 20 m/s at 60°. What is the horizontal component of velocity?
✅ v_x = v·cos60° = 20 × 0.5 = 10 m/s. Horizontal component stays constant throughout flight.
6. Which SUVAT equation should you use when time is unknown?
✅ v² = u² + 2as is the only SUVAT equation with no t — use it when time is not given and not needed.
7. An object is dropped from 80 m. How long does it take to hit the ground? (g = 10 m/s²)
✅ s = ½gt² → 80 = ½(10)t² → t² = 16 → t = 4 s.
8. At what angle should a projectile be launched for maximum range (on flat ground)?
✅ Range = u²·sin(2θ)/g is maximised when sin(2θ) = 1, i.e. 2θ = 90° → θ = 45°.
